Romanized Hokkien for People Who Read Pinyin

Some of the lyrics I romanize are a mix of Hokkien and Mandarin. This can be confusing for people who’ve only used Pinyin: Hokkien has many sounds not in Mandarin and uses a different romanization system.

This guide is meant to supplement my lyric romanizations for people who aren’t very familiar with Hokkien but would like to learn.

Intro to Hokkien Sounds

Hokkien has “b”, which is the English /b/ sound that Mandarin doesn’t have; “p”, which is the Mandarin sound /p/ that is “b” in Pinyin, which English does not have; and “ph”, which is the /pʰ/ sound that is “p” in Pinyin and English. This extends to other consonant groups: /d/ /t/ /tʰ/ and /g/ /k/ /kʰ/.

Much like other non-Mandarin languages descended from Classical Chinese, Hokkien has consonants that can be their own syllable. If you see “m” without a vowel attached, that’s pronounced “mmm”; if you see “ng” without a vowel attached, try pronouncing “ung” and dropping the “u” sound.

Hokkien also can have “ng” as the initial consonant.

Initial “g” is often silent.

Hokkien Romanization

I romanize Hokkien using Tâi-lô, which is the current system used by the Taiwanese government and popular online dictionaries like iTaigi. Prior to Tâi-lô’s creation, Pe̍h-ōe-jī (POJ) was the predominant system and is still used by some textbooks.

Other Things You Should Know

No one uses consistent Chinese characters for Hokkien words, except for dictionaries. A lot of official lyrics are partial translations into Mandarin; others use colloquial characters or Mandarin homonyms. I always romanize based off what I hear.
